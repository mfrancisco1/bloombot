Key Concepts

Understanding Electrical Current

Current is the net flow of electric charge through a material. Here's what governs it.

Conductors

Metals have a "sea" of delocalized electrons in their outer orbitals. These electrons aren't bound to any single atom — they drift freely through the lattice. When a voltage (electric field) is applied, electrons experience a net force and move, producing current. Resistance is low because the electrons encounter few obstacles.

σ ≈ 5.96 × 10⁷ S/m (copper)

Semiconductors

In semiconductors, there's a small band gap between the valence band and conduction band. At room temperature, thermal energy promotes some electrons across this gap. Doping — introducing impurity atoms — dramatically changes the number of free carriers (electrons or holes), giving us precise control over conductivity.

Band gap: ~1.1 eV (silicon)

Insulators

Insulators have a very large band gap — electrons need far more energy than is available at room temperature to jump to the conduction band. The electrons stay locked in their atomic orbitals, so essentially no current flows even when voltage is applied. This makes insulators ideal for preventing unintended current paths.

Band gap: ~9 eV (glass)

Fundamentals

Core Ideas

Current (I) The rate of charge flow, measured in amperes. One ampere = one coulomb of charge per second passing a point.
Voltage (V) The electric potential difference that drives electrons through a material — the "push" behind current.
Resistance (R) How much a material opposes current flow. Ohm's law: V = IR connects all three.
Band Gap The energy difference between valence and conduction bands — determines a material's electrical behavior.
Drift Velocity Electrons actually move very slowly (~mm/s) in a conductor. The electric field propagates near the speed of light.
Conventional vs. Electron Flow By convention, current flows from + to −. Electrons actually flow the opposite direction, from − to +.
