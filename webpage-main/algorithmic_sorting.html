Feature Structure

A proxy feature is a characteristic that isn't a protected attribute (race, age, disability) but is statistically correlated with one. When a sorting algorithm uses proxy features, it can discriminate without ever directly "seeing" group membership.

In this simulation, proxy features include employment gap, school prestige score, zip code score, and name-based callback score. These don't measure ability — they measure social circumstance, which is correlated with group membership due to historical inequities. When fed to a sorting algorithm, they produce systematic group differences in outcomes.

Final Score = (1 − proxyWeight) × LegitScore + proxyWeight × ProxyScore

LegitScore = experience×0.40 + education×0.30 + skillTest×0.30
ProxyScore = (noGap)×0.25 + schoolPrestige×0.30 + zipScore×0.25 + nameScore×0.20

Try Skills Only preset (proxy = 0%) — groups reach similar outcomes. Slide to High Proxy — the algorithm never reads "group membership" but the proxies do its work.

Even small per-stage pass-rate differences compound dramatically across multiple decision points. A system that feels "barely biased" at each step produces stark aggregate disparities at the end of the pipeline.

If Group A passes each of 4 stages at 75% and Group C at 63%:
Group A cumulative: 0.75⁴ ≈ 31.6% reach offers
Group C cumulative: 0.63⁴ ≈ 15.8% reach offers
Disparate impact ratio: 15.8 / 31.6 ≈ 0.50 — well below the 4/5 threshold (0.80)

This is the core challenge for legal attribution: the harm is distributed across steps. No single decision can be identified as "the discriminatory one" — the discrimination emerges from the system's design, not from any individual act. This is precisely why single-cause reasoning fails for diffuse harm.

Watch the Per-Stage Pass Rates table as you move the proxy slider. Notice how each stage adds a small increment of disadvantage that accumulates into a large final gap.

The weather/climate analogy is central to understanding diffuse harm. You can't attribute a single storm to climate change — but climate science shows statistically that warming makes certain storm types more frequent and intense. The individual event is noise; the population-level pattern is the signal.

In algorithmic discrimination, the same logic applies. You cannot prove that this algorithm decision discriminated against this specific applicant — their profile had many features, the threshold was opaque, perhaps they were close. But you can show that across thousands of applications, Group C applicants with equivalent true qualifications passed through at systematically lower rates. The individual path looks random. The aggregate is the evidence.

This is why Mobley v. Workday is legally difficult under current frameworks: courts remain calibrated to individual causation — "prove this decision was discriminatory" — rather than population attribution — "prove this system produces discriminatory outcomes." Computational social science provides tools for the second framing, but law has not fully caught up.

Use Trace One Agent to follow a single applicant's path. Their individual outcome looks contingent and hard to attribute. Then look at the aggregate funnel — that's the climate.

The EEOC 4/5 Rule (80% Rule) is the primary U.S. legal standard for adverse impact in employment: if the selection rate for a protected group is less than 4/5ths (80%) of the rate for the highest-selected group, this is evidence of adverse impact.

Disparate Impact Ratio = (Group C Selection Rate) / (Group A Selection Rate)
If ratio < 0.80 → potential adverse impact finding under EEOC guidelines

In Mobley v. Workday, Inc. (N.D. Cal., filed 2022), plaintiff Derek Mobley — a Black man over 40 with disabilities — applied to 80+ jobs through Workday's ATS and was rejected from all. The central legal dispute: is Workday an "employment agency" subject to Title VII and the ADEA? The 9th Circuit partially revived the case in 2024.

The deeper structural issue: even if Workday is legally covered, proving systemic algorithmic discrimination under current doctrine requires either demonstrating disparate treatment (intentional discrimination) or disparate impact with a large enough plaintiff class and clean statistical evidence. Diffuse algorithmic harm — spread across many decisions, produced by proxy features, amplified by feedback — fits neither category cleanly.

This tool lets you generate the statistical evidence a CSS researcher would produce: aggregate selection rate differentials, per-stage pass-through gaps, and disparate impact ratios — the population-level evidence that current law struggles to act on.

All agents are generated with the same true qualification drawn from an identical random distribution (group-independent). Differences in outcomes are driven entirely by feature weighting — not underlying ability. This is a key design choice to make the model's causal mechanism transparent.

The Feedback Loop models what happens when an algorithm is retrained on its own biased output. The over-representation of Group A in the hired pool shifts the effective proxy weight upward in subsequent runs, demonstrating amplification of initial bias over time — a core concern in algorithmic fairness research (Barocas & Selbst 2016; Obermeyer et al. 2019).
