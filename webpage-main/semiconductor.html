Key Concepts

How Semiconductors Work

The gap between insulator and conductor — and why that gap is so useful.

Crystal Lattice

Pure silicon forms a diamond cubic crystal structure. Each atom shares one electron with each of its four nearest neighbors through covalent bonds, creating a stable lattice with no free carriers at absolute zero.

4 valence electrons per Si atom

Band Gap

Silicon's band gap is about 1.1 eV — too large for many electrons to cross at room temperature, but small enough that thermal energy, light, or doping can push carriers into the conduction band. This "controllable conductivity" is what makes semiconductors useful.

Si: 1.1 eV · Ge: 0.67 eV · GaAs: 1.42 eV

N-Type Doping

Phosphorus has 5 valence electrons. When it replaces a silicon atom, four bond normally — the fifth is loosely bound and easily becomes a free electron. This "donates" a negative carrier, hence N-type.

Donor: 5th electron → conduction band

P-Type Doping

Boron has only 3 valence electrons. When it sits in the lattice, one bond is incomplete — creating a "hole." Nearby electrons can hop into it, making the hole drift the other direction.

Acceptor: missing bond → mobile hole

Thermal Behavior

Temperature Effects

Low Temperature (< 200 K)Very few electrons have enough thermal energy to cross the gap. The semiconductor behaves almost like an insulator.
Room Temperature (~300 K)A moderate number of electrons are thermally excited. In doped material, donor/acceptor carriers dominate.
High Temperature (> 400 K)Intrinsic carrier generation ramps up exponentially, eventually swamping the doping effect.
Carrier GenerationEach broken bond produces an electron–hole pair. Rate follows: n ∝ e−Eg/2kT.
Why This MattersEvery transistor, diode, LED, and solar cell depends on controlling which carriers are present and where they flow.
PN Junction (next step)Put N-type next to P-type and a depletion zone forms. Apply voltage → current flows one way. That's a diode.
