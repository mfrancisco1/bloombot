The Analogy Explained

Water → Electricity

Every part of the water system maps to an electrical concept. Here's the translation.

Water Pressure / Height
= Voltage (V)

The Push

Voltage is electrical pressure — the force that pushes charges through a circuit. A taller column of water exerts more pressure at the bottom, just as a higher voltage pushes more current through a wire. A battery is like a pump that lifts water back to the top.

Flow Rate (liters/sec)
= Current (I, in Amps)

The Flow

Current is the amount of charge passing a point per second. In the analogy, it's how much water flows through the hole each second. You don't "set" flow directly — it depends on pressure and restriction. More voltage, less resistance → more current.

Narrow Pipe / Small Hole
= Resistance (R, in Ohms)

The Restriction

Resistance opposes current, just as a narrow pipe slows water flow. Thin wires, long wires, and resistor components all increase resistance. The hole in the bucket acts as a variable resistor — make it smaller, and less water gets through for the same pressure.

Water Wheel
= Load (Motor, Bulb, etc.)

Doing Work

The water wheel converts water flow into mechanical motion — that's the "load" in a circuit. A light bulb, motor, or speaker converts electrical current into light, motion, or sound. More current through the load means more work done.

Bulb Brightness
= Power (P = V × I)

Energy Conversion

Power is how fast energy is converted. The bulb's brightness shows power: more voltage × more current = brighter glow. In watts, P = V × I. Double the current at the same voltage, and you double the power delivered to the load.

Pump (lifts water back up)
= Battery / Power Supply

The Source

A battery does chemical work to push electrons from low potential to high, just as a pump lifts water back to the top of the tank. Without it, the tank drains and flow stops — the circuit goes dead.
