Key Concepts

How an NPN Transistor Works

Three doped regions, two junctions, and the principle that makes all digital logic possible.

Emitter (N⁺)

Heavily doped N-type region — packed with free electrons. When the transistor turns on, these electrons are "emitted" into the base. The heavy doping ensures a large supply of carriers.

High electron density → large current capacity

Base (P)

Very thin, lightly doped P-type region. A small current here controls a much larger collector current — that's transistor action. Most emitter electrons pass straight through because the base is so thin.

Thin base → most electrons reach collector

Collector (N)

Moderately doped N-type region that "collects" the electrons that made it through the base. The collector-base junction is reverse-biased, which sweeps electrons across efficiently.

IC ≈ β × IB (β ≈ 100 typical)

As a Switch

In digital circuits, the transistor operates in two states: cutoff (no base current → switch OFF) and saturation (enough base current → switch fully ON). This is the basis of every logic gate, flip-flop, and memory cell in a computer.

Billions of switches → one CPU

Operating Modes

Three Regions

CutoffNo base current. Both junctions reverse-biased. No collector current flows. The transistor is OFF.
Active (Linear)Base-emitter forward-biased, collector-base reverse-biased. IC = β × IB. Used for amplification.
SaturationBoth junctions forward-biased. Maximum current flows. VCE drops near 0. The transistor is fully ON — a closed switch.
Current Gain (β)The ratio IC/IB. Typically 50–300 for small-signal transistors. A tiny base current controls a much larger collector current.
VBE ≈ 0.7 VThe base-emitter junction needs about 0.7V to turn on (for silicon). Below this threshold, the transistor stays in cutoff.
What's Next?Combine two transistors (NPN + PNP) into a CMOS pair and you get the building block of modern processors — low power, fast switching.
